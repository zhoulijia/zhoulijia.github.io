research


In modern statistical and machine learning applications, the number of parameters in a model can far exceed the number of training samples. In fact, many state-of-the-art results are attained by over-parameterized models that are trained with little to no regularization (such as deep neural network). At the same time, these models are powerful enough to perfectly memorize the training labels. In contrast, the traditional understanding of ML is that models with high complexity and low training error tend to overfit. With the hope of bridging the gap between classical learning theory with modern applied ML, I have been trying to understand interpolation learning through the prism of implicit regularization and uniform convergence.

  • On the implicit regularization side, I am interested in the effect of different parameterization and hyperparameter choices, such as network architecture, learning rate, minibatch size and early stopping, on the solution found by training. The idea is that when the model is over-parameterized, there usually exists infinitely many solutions that lead to small training error, but some of them can generalize better than others. It turns out that the choice of optimizer and how we optimize the loss objective is crucial to finding solutions with good generalization properties. One way to reconcile interpolation learning with bias-variance tradeoff is that SGD can find simple solutions in terms of some dimension-free complexity measure. For example, it is known that gradient methods implicitly find the interpolator with the least Euclidean norm in linear regression. Problems that I have been thinking about include the implicit regularization in early-stopped linear models, ReLU regression and matrix factorization.

  • On the uniform convergence side, the goal of my recent research is to show how a dimension-free complexity measure like Euclidean norm can be used to tightly control the population error for interpolating, or near-interpolating, linear predictors in extremly high dimensions, and to lay a principled theoretical foundation for understanding the generalization of over-parameterized non-linear models. Even though gradient methods find the minimal norm interpolator in linear regression, the norm of the minimal norm interpolator still has to diverge with the number of training samples when there is noise. As a result, establishing tight estimates for the population error requires a very precise bound with the optimal dependence on model complexity. Our papers accomplish this by a novel application of the Gaussian comparison inequality, and the technical details of our contribution can be found on the publication page.
